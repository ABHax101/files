Civil Rights Foundations
1896–1960 · from Plessy to the sit-ins
The Legal Background
  • Plessy v. Ferguson (1896): Supreme Court said segregation was constitutional — established "separate but equal" doctrine
  • De jure segregation: segregation by law (Jim Crow laws) — buses, trains, schools, restaurants, pools, parks
  • De facto segregation: segregation by custom/tradition even without laws (common in the North)
  • NAACP: founded 1909, led the legal fight to overturn segregation through the courts
  • Jackie Robinson: first Black player in modern MLB (April 15, 1947, Brooklyn Dodgers) — broke baseball's color barrier
Brown v. Board of Education (1954)

NAACP attorney Thurgood Marshall argued the case. Linda Brown was denied admission to her neighborhood school in Topeka, Kansas.

Chief Justice Earl Warren wrote the unanimous decision: "Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal." Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson. Violated the 14th Amendment's equal protection clause.

Brown II (1955): ordered desegregation to happen "with all deliberate speed" — considered a weak follow-up because it gave no firm deadline.

Little Rock, Arkansas (1957)

School board won a court order to admit 9 Black students to Central High (2,000 white students). Governor Orval Faubus sent the National Guard to block them — first time a state used armed force to defy the federal government since the Civil War.

Eisenhower sent 1,000 soldiers of the 101st Airborne Division to escort the Little Rock Nine into school. Troops stayed the whole year.

The Little Rock conflict was tied to the Brown v. Board decision — Faubus was defying that ruling.
Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–1956)
  • Rosa Parks refused to give up her bus seat on December 1, 1955
  • Boycott lasted over a year (381 days)
  • Martin Luther King Jr. (26 years old) was chosen to lead it
  • Strategy: nonviolent passive resistance, inspired by Gandhi
  • December 1956: Supreme Court declared Alabama's bus segregation laws unconstitutional
  • Led to formation of the SCLC (Southern Christian Leadership Conference) — King was first president
Sit-ins & Student Movements
  • CORE (Congress of Racial Equality, founded 1942 in Chicago): first used sit-ins — protesters sat at segregated establishments and refused to leave
  • SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee): formed at Shaw University in Raleigh, NC — sent volunteers to register voters and desegregate bus terminals
  • Freedom Riders: SNCC volunteers who rode buses into the Deep South to desegregate interstate bus terminals — met by violent white mobs in Alabama
  • Jesse Jackson participated in sit-ins as a student
CORE believed in direct nonviolent action. NAACP led the way in court action.
Challenging Segregation
1960–1965 · Kennedy, Birmingham, the big laws
Kennedy & Civil Rights

Kennedy promised to support civil rights but was initially cautious — he was worried about looking weak to Khrushchev and the USSR, and didn't want to divide the nation during the Cold War.

James Meredith (Black Air Force veteran) tried to enroll at the University of Mississippi in 1962. Governor Ross Barnett physically blocked him. Kennedy sent 500 federal marshals, then thousands of troops after riots broke out. Meredith attended class under federal guard all year.

In return for the desegregation of interstate transportation (Freedom Riders), Kennedy agreed to not prosecute the mob members who attacked the Freedom Riders — essentially a deal to keep the peace politically.
Birmingham (Spring 1963)

MLK deliberately chose Birmingham, Alabama because he knew it would provoke a violent response — and that only violence getting out of hand would force federal action.

Commissioner of Public Safety "Bull" Connor (T. Eugene Connor) ordered police to use clubs, attack dogs, and high-pressure fire hoses on demonstrators including women and children. Millions watched on TV.

King was arrested. From jail he wrote the "Letter from Birmingham Jail" — one of the most important defenses of civil disobedience ever written. Kennedy decided to introduce a new civil rights bill.

March on Washington — Aug 28, 1963

200,000+ people of all races at the Lincoln Memorial. King delivered the "I Have a Dream" speech. Organized to pressure Congress to pass the civil rights bill. Involved NAACP, SCLC, SNCC, labor unions, religious groups.

Three weeks later: Sixteenth Street Baptist Church bombing in Birmingham killed four young Black girls.

November 22, 1963: Kennedy assassinated in Dallas. LBJ becomes president and pushes hard for the civil rights bill.

The Big Laws
LawWhat It Did
Civil Rights Act of 1964Banned discrimination based on race, religion, sex. Gave federal government broad power to enforce. Title VII outlawed job discrimination by sex. Passed after an 80-day Senate filibuster by Southerners.
24th Amendment (1964)Eliminated poll taxes — fees required to vote that had been used to disenfranchise poor Black voters.
Voting Rights Act of 1965Authorized federal officials to register voters wherever local officials were blocking Black registration. Triggered by "Bloody Sunday" in Selma.
"Bloody Sunday": Marchers from Selma to Montgomery were attacked by 200+ state troopers on the Edmund Pettus Bridge. 50 hospitalized. Johnson watched on TV and proposed the Voting Rights Act 8 days later.
Urban Riots

Less than a week after LBJ signed the Voting Rights Act, the Watts Riot erupted in LA (1965). Watts, Detroit (43 dead, $50M damage, 1967), and Newark were among the worst. The difference from old race riots: now Black people were fighting back against police and white business owners in their own neighborhoods.

The Great Migration had moved millions of Black Americans from the South to Northern cities. They faced de facto segregation and poverty there — laws didn't fix that.
Black Power & MLK's End
1965–1968 · the movement fractures
Malcolm X
  • Born Malcolm Little — rough childhood, burglary conviction at 21
  • In prison, converted to the Nation of Islam — a Black nationalist religious sect that preached separation from whites
  • Became the Nation of Islam's most prominent minister after release
  • 1964: pilgrimage to Mecca → changed his views, became willing to consider limited acceptance of whites
  • February 1965: assassinated by Nation of Islam members upset with his departure from the organization
Malcolm X vs. MLK: Malcolm X initially rejected nonviolence and integration. MLK preached working within the system. Malcolm X came to represent a more militant alternative.
Black Power Movement
  • Black power: broad term — stressed racial pride, cultural distinctiveness over assimilation. Some interpreted it as justifying self-defense and even violence.
  • Cultural expressions: Afro hairstyles, African clothing, rediscovering African heritage
  • Black Panthers (1966): founded in Oakland, CA — armed organization that patrolled Black neighborhoods to protect against police brutality. Believed revolution was necessary.
  • By late 1960s the civil rights movement had fragmented into dozens of competing organizations
MLK's Assassination

April 4, 1968: King was in Memphis, Tennessee supporting a strike by Black sanitation workers. Shot by a sniper while standing on his hotel balcony.

His death triggered riots in more than 100 cities including Washington D.C. It marked the end of an era in the civil rights movement.

Kennedy / New Frontier
1961–1963 · Camelot and crisis
New Frontier Domestic Policy
  • Kennedy's legislative agenda: medical care for the poor, urban renewal, education
  • Congress (conservative coalition + Southern Dixiecrats) blocked most of it — same problem Truman had
  • Kennedy was cautious, lacked a strong voter mandate (won by only 119,000 votes)
  • Used deficit spending to boost the economy: tax cuts, higher minimum wage, increased spending
  • Peace Corps (1961): volunteer program to help poor nations in Asia, Africa, Latin America — also a Cold War tool to prevent communism
  • Alliance for Progress: aid program specifically for Latin America to reduce poverty and prevent communist revolutions
Space Race
  • 1957: USSR launched Sputnik → Eisenhower started NASA
  • 1961: USSR sent first man to space; US sent one a month later
  • Kennedy challenged the nation to reach the moon by end of the decade
  • Alan Shepard: first American in space; John Glenn: first American to orbit Earth
  • July 1969: Neil Armstrong became first man on the moon
Cold War Crises
  • Berlin Wall (1961): East Germany built the wall to stop citizens from fleeing to West Berlin. The issue: East Germans were escaping in huge numbers, embarrassing the USSR. Kennedy accepted the wall rather than risk war.
  • Cuban Missile Crisis (Oct 1962): USSR placed nuclear missiles in Cuba. 13-day standoff. Kennedy blockaded Cuba. USSR agreed to remove missiles in exchange for US pledge not to invade Cuba (and secretly removing US missiles from Turkey). Khrushchev was seen as backing down — weakened him domestically, led to his removal from power in 1964.
JFK's Assassination
Nov 22, 1963
Shot in Dallas while riding in open-air limo. Texas School Book Depository.
Arrest
Lee Harvey Oswald arrested — rifle with fingerprints, shady past (dishonorable discharge, briefly lived in USSR, supported Castro)
Days later
Jack Ruby shot and killed Oswald on live TV
Warren Commission
Investigated and concluded Oswald was the lone assassin — no conspiracy. Later Congressional investigation suggested possible other involvement.
Kennedy developed missile and submarine programs for the military. His defense buildup was a major part of the New Frontier.
LBJ & The Great Society
1963–1968 · the most ambitious domestic agenda since the New Deal
Who Was LBJ?

Middle-class Texan, served in House then Senate. FDR follower who wanted to help the common man. Legendary political persuader — known for getting in people's faces and twisting arms until he got what he wanted (the "Johnson treatment"). Had helped pass the Civil Rights Act of 1957.

The Warren Court

Chief Justice Earl Warren led the court on an activist path — interpreted constitutional rights broadly.

CaseRuling
Brown v. Board (1954)Segregated schools unconstitutional — overturned Plessy
Mapp v. Ohio (1961)Evidence obtained without a warrant cannot be used in court (exclusionary rule)
Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)Everyone has the right to an attorney (even if they can't afford one)
Miranda v. Arizona (1966)Police must inform suspects of their rights — "Miranda rights"
Hernandez v. Texas (1954)Mexican Americans have equal protection under the law — first Mexican-American civil rights case before SCOTUS
"Guns or Butter"
LBJ's biggest problem: he couldn't fund both the Great Society AND the Vietnam War. He tried to do both and ended up with a massive budget deficit. Conservatives used this to attack him. He chose not to run for re-election in 1968.
The Vietnam War
1954–1975 · America's longest war
Background
  • France controlled Vietnam until WWII, then Japan took it. After WWII France tried to take it back.
  • Ho Chi Minh: Vietnamese nationalist and communist who fought for independence
  • Domino theory: if Vietnam fell to communism, neighboring countries would too. Truman agreed to help France financially — that's why the US got involved.
  • France was defeated. Vietnam split at the 17th parallel: communist North, US-backed South
  • Also under French control in Indochina: Laos and Cambodia
Truman agreed to aid France because of the domino theory — fear that communism would spread across Southeast Asia.
US Escalation
  • Ho Chi Minh organized the Vietcong — guerilla army fighting to reunite Vietnam under communism
  • Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964): Congress gave LBJ power to "take all necessary measures" against North Vietnam after a reported attack on US ships. Only 2 votes against. Effectively handed war powers to the president.
  • Operation Rolling Thunder: sustained US bombing campaign of North Vietnam
  • By end of 1965: 180,000 US troops. By 1966: doubled.
  • During the Vietnam War, African American soldiers served in disproportionately high combat roles and suffered disproportionate casualties — the draft hit poor and minority communities hardest.
Why the US Struggled
  • Vietcong used guerrilla warfare — hit-and-run, booby traps, landmines, underground tunnels, blended with civilians
  • US used napalm (jellied gasoline bombs) and Agent Orange (chemical defoliant — stripped leaves off trees to eliminate hiding spots, destroyed farmland)
  • US went on "search and destroy" missions — trying to find and bomb enemy positions
  • LBJ had to fight a "limited war" — afraid of provoking China and the USSR to intervene (like Korea)
  • Soldiers became demoralized — alcohol, drug use, some even killed their own officers ("fragging")
Tet Offensive → Nixon
Jan 30, 1968
Tet Offensive: Vietcong launched massive coordinated surprise attack on all US airbases and South Vietnamese cities during the Vietnamese New Year. US military leaders had said the war was nearly won. This shattered that narrative.
1968
LBJ announces he won't seek re-election. Richard Nixon wins presidency promising to end the war.
May 1970
Nixon secretly orders bombing of Ho Chi Minh Trail in Cambodia to cut Vietcong supply lines. Massive public outrage when revealed.
May 4, 1970
Kent State: Ohio National Guard kills 4 students at anti-war protest. Nation is shocked.
April 30, 1975
Vietcong unify Vietnam under communism. Capital renamed Ho Chi Minh City. US involvement ends. 55,000 Americans killed, 365,000 wounded.
Nixon tried to break the peace stalemate in 1970 by invading Cambodia — this backfired massively and intensified protests at home.
Hawks vs. Doves

Hawks: wanted to escalate — bomb harder, win decisively, stop communism at all costs.

Doves: wanted to withdraw — the war was unwinnable, too costly, morally questionable. The difference was fundamentally whether the US should stay and fight or leave.

Nixon, Détente & Watergate
1969–1975 · foreign policy wins, domestic collapse
Nixon's Foreign Policy — Détente

Nixon called his approach realpolitik (or just "pragmatic realism") — deal with the world as it is, not as you wish it to be. His national security advisor was Henry Kissinger.

  • China: Nixon visited China in 1972 to open diplomatic relations. China had signaled willingness to talk by inviting the US ping-pong team to China ("ping-pong diplomacy").
  • Soviet Union: After Nixon normalized relations with China, the USSR responded by becoming more willing to negotiate — they didn't want the US and China allied against them.
  • SALT I (Strategic Arms Limitation Treaty): Nixon and Brezhnev agreed to freeze the number of nuclear missiles each side had — first major nuclear arms control agreement.
  • Nixon visited China to improve trade relations (and play the China card against the USSR).
New Federalism & Domestic Policy

New Federalism: Nixon's plan to shift power from the federal government back to states and local governments — gave states "block grants" to spend as they chose rather than specific federal programs.

This was a conservative pushback against the Great Society's expansion of federal power.
Watergate & Resignation
  • June 1972: Burglars (connected to Nixon's re-election campaign) broke into the Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate hotel in DC
  • Nixon tried to cover it up — used the CIA to block the FBI investigation
  • Senate investigation revealed Nixon had been taping all White House conversations
  • Tapes showed Nixon was involved in the cover-up from the start
  • August 9, 1974: Nixon resigned — the only US president to do so
  • Result: Deepened Americans' distrust of government. Ford pardoned Nixon, which was very controversial.
Ford & Carter
  • Ford: focused on détente with the Soviet Union. Economy was in bad shape when he left office — stagflation (high inflation + high unemployment).
  • Carter: greatest achievement was the Camp David Accords (1978) — brokered a peace deal between Egypt (Sadat) and Israel (Begin). First Arab-Israeli peace agreement.
  • Carter withdrew support from Nicaragua's dictator Somoza during a leftist (Sandinista) rebellion.
  • Iran Hostage Crisis (1979): Iranian revolutionaries seized the US embassy and held 52 Americans hostage for 444 days. Rescue mission failed. Hostages released the day Reagan was inaugurated. Important consequence: destroyed Carter's presidency and helped elect Reagan.
  • When Ford left office the economy was struggling with stagflation — high inflation + slow growth.
Other Movements
Women · Hispanic · Native American · Environment · Counterculture
Women's Rights Movement
  • Betty Friedan: wrote The Feminine Mystique (1963) — argued women were trapped in the housewife role; helped launch second-wave feminism
  • Founded NOW (National Organization for Women) — fought for equal opportunity in work and education
  • Title VII (Civil Rights Act 1964): outlawed discrimination based on sex — first federal law banning gender discrimination
  • Title IX (1972): banned sex discrimination in education — opened up sports and academic programs to women
  • ERA (Equal Rights Amendment): proposed in 1972 to guarantee gender equality in the Constitution. Phyllis Schlafly led conservative opposition. Fell 3 states short of the 38 needed. Still not ratified.
  • Roe v. Wade (1973): Supreme Court ruled abortion was a constitutional right. Overturned by Dobbs v. Jackson (2022) — court ruled the Constitution does NOT confer a right to abortion.
Hispanic Rights Movement
  • Hernandez v. Texas (1954): first Mexican-American civil rights case before SCOTUS — ruled Mexican Americans have equal protection under the law
  • Cesar Chavez: most influential Hispanic activist; fought for farm laborers (most exploited workers in the country — long hours, no benefits)
  • 1962: Chavez organized the United Farm Workers (UFW) in California
  • Strategy: nonviolent — worker strikes + consumer boycott of California grapes → forced better working conditions
  • Chicano movement: broader Hispanic political movement that led to greater representation in state, county, and city governments; pushed for ethnic pride and political power
Native American Movement

Native Americans organized activist groups due to issues including poverty, loss of tribal lands, lack of services, and broken treaty obligations.

AIM (American Indian Movement): major activist group. People with disabilities also gained expanded civil rights through legislation in this era.

Environmental Movement
  • Rachel Carson: marine biologist, wrote Silent Spring (1962) — warned about pesticides (especially DDT), sparked the modern environmental movement
  • DDT caused eggshell thinning → nearly wiped out bald eagles
  • The Cuyahoga River in Cleveland literally caught fire (1969) from industrial pollution — became a symbol of environmental crisis and helped push the Clean Air Act
  • Carson's book + activism → Earth Day (first 1970) → Congress created the EPA (Environmental Protection Agency, 1970)
  • Nixon signed: Clean Air Act (1970), Clean Water Act (1973), Endangered Species Act (1973)
  • Industrial pollution had become a problem partly because few people had anticipated the harmful side effects of industrial chemicals
  • Three Mile Island (1979): nuclear reactor meltdown in PA → US banned new nuclear plants for 25 years
  • OPEC oil embargo (1973): Arab nations cut off oil to US for supporting Israel. Gas lines, prices spiked. US realized its oil dependence.
  • Carter created the Department of Energy, pushed conservation. Congress passed the National Energy Act.
  • Ralph Nader: consumer safety activist — National Traffic and Motor Vehicle Safety Act (1966) was significant because it established federal safety standards for cars for the first time.
Counterculture

The counterculture of the 1960s/70s was partly created by the Vietnam War — young people rejected mainstream values, authority, and the military draft. Also fueled by the civil rights movement's challenge to the status quo.

College campuses became centers of protest. Kent State (1970) was a turning point — when the government kills students for protesting, it radicalizes more people.
