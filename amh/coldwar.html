Cold War Origins
1945–1947 · From allies to enemies
Why They Became Enemies
🇺🇸 USA
  • Capitalism — private ownership
  • Democracy — free elections
  • Angry USSR hid atomic bomb research
  • Angry USSR signed Nazi pact
  • Economy boomed from war
  • Had the atomic bomb
🇷🇺 USSR
  • Communism — state owns all
  • Totalitarian — one party
  • Angry US kept atom bomb secret
  • 20 million dead, devastated
  • Needed friendly neighbors
  • Had world's biggest army
The Cold War = state of hostility short of direct military confrontation between the two superpowers. No direct fighting — everything else was fair game.
Yalta Conference (Feb 1945)

FDR, Churchill, and Stalin met at a Soviet Black Sea resort. They agreed to:

  • Divide Germany into 4 occupation zones (US, UK, USSR, France)
  • Also divide Berlin the same way, even though it sat inside Soviet territory
  • Allow free elections in liberated countries
  • Create the United Nations
Stalin broke his promises immediately. Every country the Red Army occupied became a communist satellite state — Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, East Germany.
The Iron Curtain

The border between Soviet satellite states and free Western Europe. Stalin seized factories from satellite states to rebuild the USSR. Winston Churchill coined the phrase: "An iron curtain has descended across the continent."

US Response: Containment

Truman's policy: don't try to roll back communism where it already exists — just stop it from spreading. Block it with alliances and aid to weaker countries.

Truman Doctrine: US will provide economic AND military aid to any free country resisting communist takeover. First applied to Greece and Turkey (1947).

Truman's logic: he'd seen what happened when leaders appeased Hitler. He refused to repeat it with Stalin.
Who Actually Invented "Containment"?

The policy came from George F. Kennan, a Soviet expert in the State Department. He sent a famous "Long Telegram" (5,540 words) from Moscow explaining Soviet behavior, then published it anonymously as "Mr. X" in the journal Foreign Affairs.

His argument: Russia has an ancient compulsion to expand westward AND a pathological fear of the West. You can't negotiate a quick settlement with Stalin. So draw clear lines — contain the USSR to where it already is — and wait. Eventually, feeling more secure, they'll become negotiable. Cold War is preferable to a hot one that could destroy the world.

Kennan also predicted the Soviets would test American resolve, but carefully — they didn't want war any more than the US did. They just wanted as much control as they could get without war.
The 1948 Election — Truman Shocks Everyone

Truman was considered a dead man politically. The Democrat party split three ways:

  • Henry Wallace — left-wing progressives who wanted to preserve the wartime friendship with the USSR
  • Strom Thurmond — Southern "Dixiecrats" furious about Truman's civil rights moves, formed the States' Rights Party
  • Truman — the mainstream, with the odds stacked against him

Republican Thomas Dewey ran a deliberately cautious, say-nothing campaign to avoid blowing his lead. Truman attacked the "do-nothing" Republican Congress relentlessly — "Give 'em Hell, Harry!"

The Chicago Tribune printed "DEWEY DEFEATS TRUMAN" based on early returns. Truman won 303–189 electoral votes in one of the greatest upsets in US political history.

FDR's idea — a postwar international peacekeeping organization. 39 countries met in 1944 to plan it. Over the following decades it aided decolonization, helped create Israel, mediated conflicts, and provided humanitarian aid. But it quickly became an arena where the US and USSR competed for influence.

Cold War in Europe
1947–1949 · Marshall Plan, Berlin, NATO
Marshall Plan (1948)

Western Europe was devastated — food shortages, freezing winters, economic collapse. Perfect breeding ground for communism.

Congress approved ~$13 billion in grants and loans to rebuild Western Europe. Provided food, fuel, and money to restart economies.

It worked brilliantly. Rebuilt Western Europe AND created strong trading partners for the US. Aid was offered to Soviet satellites too — Stalin refused to let them take it.
Berlin Crisis & Airlift (1948–49)

Berlin was divided into 4 zones but sat deep inside Soviet East Germany. The western zones (US + UK + France) were combined into West Germany.

Stalin wanted West Berlin. In June 1948, he blocked all road, rail, and waterway access — a total blockade. Without supplies, West Berlin would have to surrender.

Stalin couldn't blockade the sky. For almost a year, US and British planes flew round-the-clock deliveries — food, fuel, medicine, clothing, even toys. At peak: a plane landed every 90 seconds.

Stalin lifted the blockade in May 1949. The airlift proved the US would go to extreme lengths to contain communism — and that Stalin could be faced down.

Korea & Asia
1949–1953 · Containment goes hot
China Falls (1949)

After Japan left China at the end of WWII, Mao Zedong's Communist forces (backed by USSR) fought Chiang Kai-shek's nationalist government (backed by $2 billion in US aid). The US aid wasn't enough. China went communist in 1949.

Americans were shocked. Containment had failed for the first time. Fear of communist expansion spiked hard.

Then in September 1949, the USSR tested its first atomic bomb. The US nuclear monopoly was over. Panic.

Korean War (1950–1953)
Post-WWII Setup
Korea divided at the 38th parallel. North = communist (Soviet-backed). South = noncommunist (US-backed).
June 25, 1950
North Korea invades South Korea. Truman sees it as a test of containment — sends troops under MacArthur plus UN forces.
Early fighting
US/South Korean forces driven back to the tip of the peninsula (Pusan Perimeter).
MacArthur's Gamble
Amphibious invasion at Inchon behind enemy lines. North Koreans collapse. US forces push back past the 38th parallel toward China.
Nov 1950
300,000 Chinese troops pour across the Yalu River. Drive US forces back below the 38th parallel.
MacArthur fired
MacArthur demands atomic weapons against China. Truman refuses — he wants a limited war, not WWIII. MacArthur goes public with his complaints. Truman fires him for insubordination.
July 27, 1953
Ceasefire signed. No victory. 50,000+ Americans dead. Border stays at 38th parallel. US troops still there today.
Red Scare & McCarthyism

With China communist and USSR with nukes, fear of communists inside the US exploded.

  • HUAC (House Un-American Activities Committee) — investigated communist influence in America, especially Hollywood
  • Rosenbergs (Julius & Ethel) — executed for allegedly passing atomic secrets to the Soviets
  • Alger Hiss — State Department official convicted of perjury (accused of being a Soviet spy)
  • Senator Joseph McCarthy — made endless unsupported claims of communists in the US government. McCarthyism = making accusations without evidence
McCarthy finally went too far when he accused the US Army of harboring communists. The Army-McCarthy hearings were televised. The country saw him as a bully. He was censured by the Senate in 1954.
Alger Hiss

A State Department official and FDR aide at Yalta. In 1948 a former communist named Whitaker Chambers accused Hiss of funneling classified documents to the USSR. Most Republicans were ready to drop it — until Richard Nixon pursued the case obsessively.

Nixon produced microfilms suggesting Hiss had retyped classified documents, and poked holes in Hiss's story under questioning. Hiss was convicted of perjury. Nixon's career was launched. Americans wondered: how many other "bright New Dealers" were spies?

The Rosenbergs & Project Venona

Julius and Ethel Rosenberg — a New York Jewish couple and Communist Party members — were arrested in 1950, charged with heading a Soviet spy ring that passed atomic bomb secrets to the USSR. They denied everything. Many believed they were victims of anti-communist hysteria.

They were executed in June 1953. The controversy raged for decades — were they really guilty?

Project Venona: Since 1946, American cryptographers had secretly cracked Soviet spy codes and were reading ~3,000 messages between Moscow and the US. The messages confirmed the Rosenbergs' guilt — but the government couldn't reveal this without tipping off the Soviets that their codes were broken. The government declassified Project Venona in 1995, finally providing solid public evidence.
  • 1949: USSR tests atomic bomb. US monopoly ends.
  • 1952: US tests hydrogen bomb — 67x more powerful than Hiroshima bomb (10.4 million tons of TNT)
  • 1953: USSR tests hydrogen bomb
  • Both sides built ICBMs (intercontinental ballistic missiles) — nukes deliverable anywhere on Earth
  • Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD): if either side launches, both sides are destroyed. So neither side launches.
  • Eisenhower's strategy: massive retaliation — any communist aggression met with overwhelming nuclear force. Also called brinkmanship — go to the brink of war to make them back down.
Cold War Goes Global
1953–1961 · CIA, Space Race, Kennedy
Eisenhower's CIA Strategy

Brinkmanship could stop a Soviet invasion but couldn't stop communist revolutions within countries. Eisenhower's solution: covert CIA operations to secretly overthrow anti-American leaders.

  • Iran (1953): CIA helped overthrow PM Mossadegh (who nationalized oil) and reinstated the Shah (pro-US). Backfired long-term — anti-US sentiment fueled the 1979 Iranian Revolution.
  • Guatemala (1954): CIA overthrew elected President Árbenz (who nationalized land from United Fruit Company). Replaced with military dictator.
Many developing countries blamed US capitalism for their problems and looked to the USSR as a model for industrialization. The CIA operations made this worse.
Khrushchev Takes Over

Stalin died in 1953. Nikita Khrushchev emerged as Soviet leader. He accused the US of a "feverish arms race" but also said "we will bury capitalism… your grandchildren will live under communism."

Eisenhower tried to set up a summit meeting to ease tensions. Just before it happened, the USSR shot down a US U-2 spy plane over Soviet airspace. Summit canceled. Relations cratered.

Hungary Uprising (1956)

Khrushchev gave a secret speech to Soviet leaders attacking Stalin's policies and saying there were "many roads to communism." The CIA got a copy and broadcast it across Eastern Europe. It sparked riots.

Anti-Soviet Hungarians rebelled and took control of Budapest. The Soviets hesitated — watching to see what the US would do. Dulles had been loudly promising to "roll back" communism and help Eastern Europeans who rebelled.

Eisenhower and Dulles did nothing. Soviet tanks rolled into Budapest and crushed the revolution. The episode shattered confidence in Dulles's bold rhetoric worldwide. Hungary remained a satellite state.
Suez Crisis (1956)

Eisenhower wanted Arab nations to align with the US, not the USSR. The US offered to help Egypt build the Aswan Dam on the Nile. When Egypt bought weapons from communist Czechoslovakia, Congress forced Dulles to withdraw the offer.

Egypt responded by seizing the Suez Canal from the Anglo-French company that controlled it, planning to use its revenues to fund the dam. Britain and France invaded Egypt in 1956.

Eisenhower was furious — his allies had acted without telling him. The USSR threatened rocket attacks on Britain and France. Eisenhower put US nuclear forces on alert and forced Britain and France to back down. But the damage was done: the USSR won a major diplomatic victory and Arab nations began accepting Soviet aid.
Eisenhower's Farewell Address

In 1961 Eisenhower warned Americans about the growing influence of the military-industrial complex — the dangerous alliance between the military establishment and the defense industry. He worried that in a democracy, this combination could distort national priorities and foreign policy.

"I wish I could say that a lasting peace is in sight."

Kennedy replaced Eisenhower's all-or-nothing nuclear approach with flexible response — a range of options:

  • Larger conventional (non-nuclear) army
  • Increased foreign economic aid
  • Peace Corps — young Americans doing humanitarian work in developing countries (also great soft power vs. communism)
Cuba & Berlin
1961–1963 · The hottest moments
Castro Takes Cuba (1959)

Fidel Castro overthrew the US-backed Batista government and established a Communist regime with Soviet ties. Cuba is 90 miles from Florida. Americans were furious and terrified.

Bay of Pigs (April 1961)

CIA plan inherited from Eisenhower: train ~1,400 Cuban exiles in Florida, land them at the Bay of Pigs, trigger a popular uprising to overthrow Castro.

Total disaster. Castro's forces killed or captured almost all of the invaders. Kennedy had approved the operation but refused to provide US air cover at the last minute — best of both bad options. Humiliated the US and Kennedy personally. Made Castro more popular.
Berlin Wall (1961)

Thousands of East Germans were escaping to the West through Berlin every month. Khrushchev demanded the US/UK/France leave West Berlin. Kennedy refused.

Khrushchev's response: on August 13, 1961, East Germany began building the Berlin Wall — literally overnight, barbed wire then concrete. Guards shot anyone trying to escape.

The Berlin Wall stood for 28 years as the most visible symbol of the Cold War. It fell November 9, 1989.
Cuban Missile Crisis (Oct 1962)

The closest the world came to nuclear war since WWII.

Oct 16, 1962
US spy planes photograph Soviet nuclear missile sites being built in Cuba. Long-range missiles — could hit most of the US.
Oct 22
Kennedy goes on national TV to announce the missiles and demand their removal. Orders a naval blockade of Cuba to stop more deliveries.
6 tense days
Soviet ships headed toward Cuba. Nuclear war seemed imminent. Behind the scenes, frantic negotiations.
Resolution
Deal: USSR removes Cuban missiles. US promises not to invade Cuba. (Secret deal: US also removed missiles from Turkey.) Crisis over.
Aftermath: both sides scared themselves into installing a direct "hotline" between Washington and Moscow. The crisis led to the first arms control talks.
Kennedy Assassinated (Nov 22, 1963)

Kennedy was shot in his motorcade in Dallas by Lee Harvey Oswald. The nation was devastated. Lyndon B. Johnson was sworn in aboard Air Force One.

The Home Front
1950s · Boom, suburbs, and fear
Postwar Economic Boom

WWII ended the Depression and supercharged the economy. Veterans came home with money from service pay and war bonds — $135 billion in savings. They bought everything.

  • Highest standard of living in the world by end of the 1950s
  • Cold War defense spending kept the economy humming
  • Marshall Plan created overseas markets for US exports
  • First credit card created 1950
  • TV, cars, appliances — consumerism exploded
Baby Boom & Suburbs
  • Returning soldiers married and had babies — 1 baby born every 7 seconds in 1957
  • GI Bill: cheap home loans and college tuition for veterans → millions moved to suburbs
  • Levittown, NY: prototype suburb — cookie-cutter affordable houses for white families
  • Interstate Highway Act (1956): Congress built 41,000 miles of expressways — enabled suburban commuting
  • First enclosed, air-conditioned mall: Southdale Shopping Center, Minnesota
"White flight": suburbs were mostly white. Black families left behind in cities as white families fled. Deepened racial segregation.
Car Culture & TV
  • Cars boomed — suburbs required them. Big, flashy, powerful cars.
  • Drive-in movies, drive-thru restaurants
  • TV ownership: 9% in 1950 → 90% by 1960 (45 million TVs)
  • Rock 'n' roll emerged from Black artists — Elvis Presley made it mainstream. Parents were horrified ("it's immoral!")
  • Teenagers became a consumer market for the first time — music, movies, clothes targeted at teens
Conformity & Its Cracks

TV/movies pushed a conformist ideal: man as breadwinner, woman as housewife, suburban family, white picket fence. "Keeping up with the Joneses."

  • Reality: ~40% of mothers had jobs
  • Alfred Kinsey: sexologist revealed premarital sex and affairs were common — shocked Americans
  • Beat Movement ("Beatniks"): artists/writers (Jack Kerouac) rejecting conformity, suburbs, and consumerism. Inspired the 1960s counterculture/hippies.
  • Early civil rights challenges: Jackie Robinson (1947), Brown v. Board of Education (1954), MLK emerges (1955)
Nuclear Fear at Home

When USSR tested the H-bomb in 1953, everyday Americans prepared for nuclear attack:

  • School "duck-and-cover" drills — students hid under desks (wouldn't actually help, but…)
  • Families built backyard fallout shelters stocked with canned food
  • Civil defense sirens installed in cities
The fear was real. Both sides had enough nukes to destroy each other many times over. MAD was the only thing keeping the peace.
Civil Rights
1950s · The movement begins
The Legal Foundation: Plessy → Brown

Since 1896 (Plessy v. Ferguson), "separate but equal" was constitutional law. In practice it meant segregated everything — schools, buses, water fountains, waiting rooms, even Bibles in courtrooms. Former slave states plus Oklahoma had full segregation laws on the books.

Thurgood Marshall, the NAACP's lead attorney, argued before the Supreme Court that separate facilities were inherently unequal — segregation itself told Black children they were inferior, damaging them psychologically in ways that could never be undone.

Brown v. Board of Education (1954): The Supreme Court unanimously agreed. Chief Justice Earl Warren wrote that segregating children "solely because of their race generates a feeling of inferiority… that may affect their hearts and minds in a way unlikely ever to be undone." Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson.

The case started with 8-year-old Linda Brown, who was denied access to an elementary school 5 blocks from her home in Topeka, Kansas — her Black school was 21 blocks away.

Little Rock (1957)

When the first Black students tried to enroll at Central High School in Little Rock, Arkansas, an angry white mob met them with rocks and curses. Governor Orval Faubus called out the National Guard to prevent their enrollment — claiming he was "keeping the peace."

Eisenhower personally disliked the Brown decision and thought it was a mistake. But Faubus was defying federal law.

Eisenhower federalized the Arkansas National Guard — overnight the troops' mission reversed from blocking the students to protecting them. He later said appointing Earl Warren as Chief Justice was "the biggest damned-fool mistake I ever made."
Rosa Parks & the Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955)

In December 1955, Rosa Parks, a Black secretary, refused to give up her seat on a Montgomery, Alabama bus to a white man as city law required. She was arrested.

Martin Luther King Jr., a young preacher, became the spokesman for a Black boycott of the Montgomery bus system. King's house was bombed. From the wreckage of his front porch, he explained his strategy: nonviolent civil disobedience.

The logic: refuse to obey unjust laws, but without violence. When police brutalized peaceful protesters, decent people watching on TV and in the news would demand change. King was right — the strategy worked.

King founded the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC) to lead the movement. In 1960, the SCLC's youth arm, the SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee), organized sit-ins at segregated lunch counters.

Jackie Robinson & Truman's Role
  • 1947: Jackie Robinson integrated professional baseball — broke the color barrier in America's most popular sport
  • 1948: Truman signed an Executive Order banning racial discrimination in the military and civil service — desegregated the armed forces
  • Truman's civil rights moves enraged Southern Democrats (the "Dixiecrats") and contributed to the 3-way party split in 1948
